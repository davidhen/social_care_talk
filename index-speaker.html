What is social care?

  • Often referred to as “Long-term care”

  • No accepted definition or measurement (a bit like multimorbidity)

Long defintion

“Adult Social Care”… includes all forms of personal care and other practical assistance provided for individuals who, for reasons of age, illness, disability, pregnancy, childbirth, dependency on alcohol or drugs, or any other similar circumstances, are in need of such care or other assistance…” 1

  1. The Health and Social Care Act 2012 c7, part 3, chapter 1, Section 65, Subsection 4

Longer definition

“… a range of services required by persons with a reduced degree of functional capacity, physical or cognitive, and who are consequently dependent for an extended period of time with basic activities of daily living (ADL). This ”personal care” component is frequently provided in combination with help with basic medical services such as ”nursing care” (wound dressing, pain management, medication, health monitoring), as well as prevention, rehabilitation or palliative care. Long term care services can also be combined with lower-level care related to”domestic help” or help with instrumental activities of daily living (IADL).” 1

  1. OECD/EU (2013) A good life in old age? Monitoring and improving quality in long-term care. https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/a-good-life-in-old-age_9789264194564-en

International models of social care

Universal models

  • Access not determined by income (user or family)
  • Therefore wide uptake
  • User out-of-pocket payments are still a feature!!!
    • Varies from country to country (4% in Sweden 33% in Korea)
  • Income may be assesses in some cost-sharing models
  • More expensive than other systems
    • OECD average 1.5% GDP
    • Universal systems up to 3.6% GDP (2011)

International models of social care

Universal models - the holy grail?

“… the separation between health and long-term care budgets in all these countries but Belgium, generally reduces utilisation of more expensive health care services and professionals (e.g. hospital care, doctors) for long-term care needs, for example by making”social hospitalisation” of frail people with LTC needs more difficult”

No evidence to back this up!!

International models of social care

Mixed systems

  • Highly heterogenous
  • Characterised by lack of single-programme LTC system and range of LTC benefits
    • Parallel universal schemes: e.g. Scotland Free personal care at home and in institution but means-tested accommodation charges in care homes and non PC care charges at home.
    • Income-related universal benefit: e.g. France, Ireland. All assessed as eligible get public benefit but amount is adjusted according to means
    • Mix of universal and means-tested care: Universal coverage of skilled nursing care OR personal care at home (not both)

International models of social care

Means-tested safety net

  • Income and/or assets are used to set threshold for eligibility to publicly-funded LTC – only those falling below the thresholds receive funds
  • United States
  • Also England but:
    • Attendance Allowance
    • Universal Credit (previously DLA, PIP)
    • Health component free (Good if cancer, Bad if dementia)
  • Generally thought to result in unmet need and leaves some families above the threshold vulnerable to catastrophic care costs (1 in 10 in England)

International models of social care

  • Rising demand for LTC and expectation of quality
  • Unlikely supply of unpaid care will meet this demand = ↑demand for formal health and social care services and/or unmet need which = ↓ QoL
  • Funding models are essential to patterns of care receipt via (dis)incentivisation.
  • State bears the cost for highest need with lowest resources in all state. What happens with the rest is what matters.

Social care in the UK

  • Social care is a devolved matter
  • England, Scotland and Wales all employ thresholds for care access
  • Data on Northern Ireland is hard to come by
  • Property is only adjudicated an asset with reference to residential care
  • Wales has a more generous threshold than other nations
  • Personal Care is free in Scotland
  • Rates of receipt of social care have reduced in all home nations since 2010
  • Austerity has created major difficulties for Local Authorities
